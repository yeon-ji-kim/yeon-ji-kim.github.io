Overview

It is difficult for non-experts to build machine learning (ML) models at the level that satisfies their needs. Deep learning models are even more challenging because it is unclear how to improve the model, and a trial-and-error approach is not feasible since training these models is time-consuming. To assist these novice users, we examined how interactive and explainable feedback while training a deep learning network can contribute to model performance and users‚Äô satisfaction, focusing on the data preparation process.

We conducted a user study with 31 participants without expertise and while no significant performance gain was observed, we identified potential barriers during the process and found that interactive and explainable feedback provides complementary benefits for improving users‚Äô understanding of ML.

Process

Research Question

Studies have shown that it is not straightforward for non-experts to build ML models without prior knowledge and that they cannot build ML models in an efficient way.

The major problems were:

  • Non-experts rarely tried to understand the internal mechanism of learning algorithms.
  • Most of them did not consider the overfitting problem.
Hence they had trouble improving the performance or even giving up their tasks or ended up having poor accuracy on a new dataset.


"How can we better assist non-experts with building a deep learning model?"

Our Approach

To better assist novice users with building a deep learning model, we decided to use the concept of Interactive Machine Learning and Explainable Machine Learning from previous research.

Our definition for each concept:

üè∑Ô∏è Interactivity
Allowing users to quickly examine the impact of their actions and adapt subsequent inputs to obtain desired behavior when training ML models.

üè∑Ô∏è Explainability
Uncovering the process of training or outcomes of an ML system with explanations presented in forms that are easy to understand for humans.

Tool

To observe how non-experts create ML systems and test the effectiveness of our approach, we decided to create an environment for building an ML model.

Key Challenge: (1) Help novices build machine learning models easily and (2) include interactive and explainable features.
Approach: Let users focus on easier processes: "preparing dataset" and "evaluating models".

User Study

To investigate the effectiveness of interactivity and explainability and to identify potential barriers that non-experts would experience while building their ML systems, we conducted a study with our custom user interface designed for this study.

Participants
31 participants who had little or no ML-related experience.

Data Condition
Image dataset with 3 classes related to dogs (Bulldog, Yorkshire, Labrador).

Task
Build a machine learning model with higher accuracy than that of the initial model as high as possible.

Procedure

Data & Analysis

With the non-parametric data collected from participants, I analyzed them qualitatively and quantitatively using Python and Excel/Google Spreadsheet.

For subjective responses, I set several meaningful behaviors and categorized each participant into themes if they showed such behavior.

Findings

01

Understanding of Novices

The Understanding of Building a Better ML Model

  • All participants' understanding of constructing an ML model has been improved after performing the task.
  • Understanding has increased regardless of the data type, in both image and speech.

Before the task

"You should save characteristics of a person's eyes, nose, and mouth."

After the task

"Rather than saving only the face data, it would be nice to save the whole body and gait information. It seems that accuracy may decrease due to lack of variety in data if only the face is recognized."

02

Feedback Features

The Use and Subjective Assessments of Feedback Features

We further assessed how each feature for each feedback type assisted participants in preparing a dataset for building ML models by analyzing participants‚Äô subjective feedback such as contribution (i.e., how much this feature has contributed to their decision when preparing the data) and usefulness at the end of the study.

03

Observed Behaviors

Perceived Task Loads and Observed Behaviors of Novices

Perceived Task Load

When compared to the responses collected from experts, we have confirmed that mental demand is slightly higher for participants. On the other hand, other responses were similar, including effort. This suggests that novice users‚Äô perceived task load is not too different from experts except for mental demand.

Amount of Data Novices Prepared

The number of images that participants used for training varied. More than half of the participants started with 20-40 images per class and maintained the number throughout the task (N = 17) while the other 6 participants gradually increase or decrease the number of training data. This is contrary to the behavior of experts who started training the model with a large volume of data. In the study, only the remaining eight added images as many as possible from the start, assuming that adding many images would increase the accuracy, especially for F8, who related the performance with the word ‚Äòbig data.‚Äô

Tendency to Over-clean the Data

To understand users‚Äô mental models, we observed how participants built their ML models while they are performing the task. As a result, we found that participants had a tendency to clean the data as much as possible without knowing that this can cause an overfitting problem. To be specific, participants tended to remove images if dogs with different breeds or people are included for the selected class even if the correct dog is present. This behavior was more frequently observed from the participants in the default condition, which had the minimum feedback without interactive nor explainable feedback. Moreover, when adding new pictures, most participants added images if and only if the images are representative of that class, such as images where the frontal face of a single dog with the correct breed is visible. This indicates that non-expert users believe that having only the representative data can lead to higher accuracy without knowing that their model would not perform well on real-world data with large variations (noises).

Reflections

What can be improved

To present novices an explanation of how a machine learning model perceive images, we initially planned on having a feature visualization. However, implementing a fully explainable deep learning model was difficult even for many researchers. Instead, we decided to visualize images with activation maps, which also helped users to understand the underlying process of machine learning. Improving features in the application to provide explanations of the decisions the model made would help novices understand the system better.

Working on a Research Project for the First Time

Working on a research project, and even leading a team of two, required being precise in designing methodology, writing a tool guide for participants, and managing all the data from the study. I realized that there are many kinds of unexpected variables that can happen during the experiment. Do not try to predict users! After many iterations of pilot studies, I could see how users utilize the system, which may have been different from our purpose. But that was the most exciting part of the user research and I learned how to interact with and observe actual users.
